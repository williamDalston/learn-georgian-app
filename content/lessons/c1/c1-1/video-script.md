**Level:** C1
**Duration:** 70 minutes

## Learning Objectives
By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:
- Form relative clauses using wh-phrases with -c enclitic (romel-ma=c)
- Use complementizer rom in relative clauses
- Create non-finite relative clauses with participles
- Understand nuances between different relative clause types
- Distinguish between restrictive and non-restrictive relative clauses

## Key Concepts
- Relative clauses
- Question words with -c ending (wh-phrases)
- The word rom (meaning 'that')
- Participles
- Complex sentence structure
- Restrictive vs non-restrictive

### Introduction: What Are Relative Clauses? (0:40 - 3:00)
**[Show diagram: Simple sentence → Complex sentence with relative clause]**

"Before we jump into Georgian specifics, let's make sure we understand what relative clauses are. A relative clause is a subordinate clause that modifies a noun - it gives us more information about that noun.

For example, take these two simple sentences:
- 'I met the woman.'
- 'She speaks Georgian.'

We can combine them using a relative clause:
- 'I met the woman WHO speaks Georgian.'

In English, we use relative pronouns like 'who,' 'which,' 'that,' and 'whose.' Georgian has multiple ways to form relative clauses, and today we'll explore three powerful patterns:
1. Question words with the -c ending (wh-phrases)
2. The word rom (which means 'that')
3. Verb forms called participles

Each has its own uses. Let's begin with the most common pattern."

---

### Main Content Section 1: Wh-Phrases with -c Enclitic (3:00 - 25:00)
**[Show wh-phrases chart on screen]**

"Georgian forms relative clauses using interrogative pronouns - the same words you use for asking questions. These become relative pronouns when you add the enclitic -c.

Let me show you the wh-phrases:

**Common Wh-Phrases:**
- რომელი (romeli) - which/what
- რა (ra) - what
- ვინ (vin) - who
- სადაც (sadac) - where
- როდესაც (rodesac) - when
- როგორ (rogor) - how

**The -c Enclitic:**
When used in relative clauses, these words often combine with -c (კ or ც). Sometimes they already include it, like სადაც (sadac = სად + -c).

Let's look at basic examples:

**[Show example sentences on screen]**

**Example 1 - რომელი + -c:**
- ის ქალბატონი, რომელმაც გამარჯობა მითხრა, ქართველია.
- is kalbat'oni, romel-mac gamarjoba mitkhra, kartvelia.
- 'The woman WHO greeted me is Georgian.'

Notice: რომელმაც (romel-mac = romeli + mac). The -mac is the ergative case marker plus the -c enclitic.

**Example 2 - რა + -c:**
- წიგნი, რაც გუშინ წავიკითხე, ძალიან საინტერესო იყო.
- ts'igni, rac gushin ts'avik'itkhe, dzalian saint'ereso iq'o.
- 'The book WHICH I read yesterday was very interesting.'

For direct objects and in nominative contexts, we use რაც (ra-c).

Let's practice identifying the patterns..."

### Practice Exercise 1 (25:00 - 30:00)
**[Interactive breakdown of complex relative clause]**

"Let's break down a complex relative clause together:

**[Show sentence on screen, highlight each part]**
ამ კაცმა, რომელმაც ქართული ენა შემოგვახასიათა, საქართველოში 20 წელი ცხოვრობს.
am katsma, romel-mac kartuli ena shemogvakhasiata, Sakartveloshi 20 ts'eli tskhovrobs.

Step by step:
1. ამ კაცმა (am katsma) = 'this man' (ergative case because it's the subject of the main verb)
2. რომელმაც... (romel-mac...) = relative pronoun 'who' (ergative, agreeing with the noun)
3. ქართული ენა შემოგვახასიათა (kartuli ena shemogvakhasiata) = 'explained Georgian to us'
4. საქართველოში 20 წელი ცხოვრობს (Sakartveloshi 20 ts'eli tskhovrobs) = 'lives in Georgia for 20 years'

Translation: 'This man, WHO explained Georgian to us, has been living in Georgia for 20 years.'

Notice how the relative pronoun agrees in case with the noun it refers to. The main verb's subject (ამ კაცმა) is ergative, so the relative pronoun is also ergative: რომელმაც."

### Main Content Section 2: Complementizer rom (30:00 - 45:00)
**[Show rom construction pattern]**

"The second major way to form relative clauses in Georgian is using the complementizer რომ (rom) - 'that' or 'which.'

რომ-clauses are extremely common and often preferred in more formal or literary contexts. Here's the pattern:

**[Formula on screen: Noun + rom + clause]**

**Example 1 - Direct Object:**
- წიგნი, რომ წავიკითხე, ძალიან საინტერესო იყო.
- ts'igni, rom ts'avik'itkhe, dzalian saint'ereso iq'o.
- 'The book THAT I read was very interesting.'

**Example 2 - Subject:**
- ის კაცი, რომ ქართულად ლაპარაკობს, პედაგოგია.
- is katsi, rom kartulad laparak'obs, pedagogia.
- 'That man WHO speaks Georgian is a teacher.'

Notice: With rom, the verb takes the short form (no auxiliary verb in present tense).

**[Show comparison table]**

"Let's compare rom with wh-phrases:

**რაც (ra-c) vs რომ (rom):**
- რაც tends to be more emphatic or specific
- რომ is more neutral and common
- Both are correct, but there are subtle pragmatic differences

**Another rom example:**
- ქალაქი, რომ ვცხოვრობ, ძალიან მშვენიერია.
- k'alak'i, rom vtskhovrob, dzalian mshvenieria.
- 'The city WHERE I live is very beautiful.'

Here, rom handles the 'where' meaning without needing a specific 'where' word."

### Main Content Section 3: Non-Finite Participles (50:00 - 65:00)
**[Introduce participle concept visually]**

"And now we come to what I consider one of the most elegant features of Georgian syntax: non-finite relative clauses using participles.

Georgian participles are verb forms that function like adjectives. They can create extremely concise and sophisticated relative clauses.

**[Show participle formation]**

**Present Active Participle:**
Remove the person marker and add -ი (-i) or -ული (-uli)

Example: წერა (ts'era = 'to write')

Present forms:
- წავწერ (ts'avts'er) - 'I write'
- წერილი (ts'erili) - 'writing (act of)'

Participle: მწერი (mts'eri) - 'writing / who writes'

**[Show examples on screen]**

**Example 1:**
- მწერი კაცი ჩემი მეგობარია.
- mts'eri katsi chemi megobaria.
- 'The writing man is my friend.' / 'The man WHO WRITES is my friend.'

- ჩემს წერილს წერა კაცი.
- chems ts'erils ts'era katsi.
- 'The man WHO IS WRITING my letter.'

**Example 2 - Past Participle:**
To form past passive participles, add -ილი (-ili) or -ული (-uli):

- წაკითხული წიგნი
- ts'ak'itkhuli ts'igni
- 'The read book' / 'The book THAT WAS READ'

**[Show more examples]**

**Passive Participle Examples:**
- გამოცემული წიგნი (gamotsemuli ts'igni) - 'the published book'
- გაწაფული სტუდენტი (gats'ap'uli st'udent'i) - 'the trained student'
- ჩაწერილი სიმღერა (chats'erili simghra) - 'the recorded song'

These participles create elegant, concise relative clauses without needing complex structures.

**Example Sentence:**
ამ წაკითხულ წიგნში ბევრი საინტერესო ინფორმაციაა.
am ts'ak'itkhul ts'ignshi bevri saint'ereso inpormatsiaa.
'In this READ book, there is much interesting information.'

This is far more elegant than: 'In this book, WHICH WE READ...'"

### Summary (65:00 - 68:00)
**[Review all three patterns]**

"Let's summarize what we've learned today about Georgian relative clauses:

**Three Major Patterns:**

1. **Wh-phrases with -c:**
   - რომელმაც (romel-mac) - 'who/which' (ergative)
   - რაც (rac) - 'which/what' (nominative/dative)
   - Case-agreement with the head noun
   - Very common in spoken Georgian

2. **Complementizer rom:**
   - Simply რომ (rom)
   - More neutral, formal
   - No case agreement needed
   - Preferred in literary Georgian

3. **Non-finite participles:**
   - Elegant and concise
   - მწერი (mts'eri) - 'writing/who writes'
   - წაკითხული (ts'ak'itkhuli) - 'read/which was read'
   - Creates sophisticated, literary style

**When to use which:**
- Wh-phrases: Emphasis, specificity, everyday speech
- რომ: Neutral, formal contexts
- Participles: Literary, concise, advanced writing

All three are correct - the choice depends on context, register, and personal style."
